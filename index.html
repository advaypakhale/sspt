    By Advay Pakhale and Milton Lee

Singapore's Non-Inclusive Society for the Visually-Impaired

We shed the light on this much ignored situation in Singapore.

Significance of The Issue


Who are the visually impaired?

The visually impaired are those suffering from medical conditions which reduce their ability to see, that is unable to be corrected by conventional means. This is a group which includes the blind, a group which lacks the ability to see completely, as well as those with extremely poor vision. The cut-off threshold for many first-world countries, like the USA and the UK, is a visual acuity of 2/20 with the best possible correction. Such a person would need to be closer than 2 meters in order to see details that a person with good eyesight could make out at 20 meters. The important clause here is best possible correction- even with glasses or contact lenses, their visual acuity remains below 2/20. As such, these people’s lives are negatively impacted by their poor visual acuity, meaning that many resort to using methods such as seeing-eye dogs and walking sticks in a bid to mitigate the negative effects of disabilities.


How is the situation in Singapore like?

Singapore is home to about 140,000 people with sensory disabilities, a statistic that includes both the hearing and visually-impaired, of which approximately 70,000 will be visually-impaired. This is approximately 1.5% of Singapore’s 3.9 million-strong resident population! However, in spite of the sizeable number of visually-impaired people in Singapore, visually-impaired people still face a number of challenges in society, such as a lack of blind-friendly facilities islandwide, and poor available employment opportunities.

Visually-impaired people rely primarily on their senses of touch and hearing to navigate the world around them. Thus, systems such as tactile-paving are extremely important for accommodating the needs of the visually-impaired. Despite this, though tactile-paving is commonplace around public pedestrian footpaths, and within train stations, many private enterprises, such as shopping centres and retail outlets, do not have blind-accommodating systems in place, meaning that visually-impaired people have difficulties navigating them alone.

In addition, a stigma is attached to the work effectiveness of the visually-impaired, resulting in few job opportunities for the blind- just 275 job vacancies were listed for disabled persons by the Open Door Fund in 2012, a fund dedicated to securing jobs for the disabled. This prevents visually-disabled people from supporting themselves as easily, forcing them to rely on others and perpetuating a cycle of dependency.

As can be seen from the above, Singapore is not a blind-friendly society. This needs to change.
